Prostate Cancer

SBRT: 5 sessions.
No surgery. No catheter.

Prostate cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in Singaporean men — and most of it can be treated without surgery. SBRT (Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy) delivers a curative dose in just five outpatient sessions, with no incision and no hospital stay.

Why SBRT for prostate cancer?

A precision alternative to surgery.

SBRT (Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy) uses image-guided high-dose radiation, shaped tightly around the prostate, to ablate cancer cells. Long-term clinical trial data — including 5- and 10-year follow-up — confirms that for low- and intermediate-risk prostate cancer, SBRT delivers cancer control outcomes comparable to surgery, with significantly fewer side effects.

The benefits patients value most

  • 5 sessions instead of 30+ — completed in 1–2 weeks instead of 6–8
  • No surgery, no catheter — entirely outpatient, no hospital stay
  • Lower risk of incontinence and erectile dysfunction compared to radical prostatectomy
  • Resume normal life immediately — most patients work through treatment
  • Equivalent cancer control to surgery in low- and intermediate-risk disease

Pre-diagnosis: symptoms & screening

Many early prostate cancers cause no symptoms — which is why screening matters. The Singapore Urological Association recommends men aged 50 and above (40 with family history) discuss PSA testing with their GP.

Possible symptoms include difficulty urinating, weak urine flow, frequent night-time urination, blood in urine or semen, persistent lower back or pelvic pain, and erectile dysfunction. Most of these symptoms are also caused by benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) — which is much more common — but warrant evaluation.

Early-stage prostate cancer (Stage 1–2)

If your prostate cancer is confined to the prostate gland (Stage 1 or 2), three main curative options exist:

  • Active surveillance — for low-risk, slow-growing disease, careful monitoring may be appropriate
  • Radical prostatectomy — surgical removal of the prostate
  • Radiation therapy — including SBRT (5 sessions), conventional IMRT (35–40 sessions), or brachytherapy (seed implant)

For low- and intermediate-risk disease, randomised data shows SBRT achieves the same cancer control as surgery with fewer urinary and sexual side effects. Your specialist will help you weigh the trade-offs for your case.

Locally advanced prostate cancer (Stage 3)

If the cancer extends beyond the prostate capsule but has not spread to distant sites, a combination of radiation therapy and 6–24 months of androgen deprivation therapy (hormone therapy) is the standard curative approach. Long-term cure rates with this combination exceed 70%.

Stage 4 / metastatic prostate cancer

Even when prostate cancer has spread, treatment can offer long disease-free intervals and good quality of life. For oligometastatic disease (1–5 sites of spread), SBRT to each metastasis — combined with systemic therapy — has been shown to delay progression and improve survival in randomised trials.

SpaceOAR Hydrogel

A gel that protects the rectum during radiation.

SpaceOAR is a soft, biodegradable hydrogel injected between the prostate and the rectum before SBRT begins. It creates roughly 1 cm of space — moving the rectal wall away from the high-dose radiation zone — and dramatically reduces the risk of long-term bowel side effects. The gel is fully reabsorbed by the body within six months.

SpaceOAR is offered as part of every AARO prostate SBRT pathway when clinically indicated. It is a 30-minute outpatient procedure performed under sedation.

FAQ

The questions men ask most often.

For appropriately selected patients with low- or intermediate-risk prostate cancer, SBRT offers equivalent cancer cure rates to surgery — with no incision, no catheter, no hospital stay, and lower rates of long-term incontinence. Surgery remains an excellent option for some patients; the right choice depends on your tumour, anatomy, and preferences.

Most patients with localised prostate cancer (Stage 1–2) and many with early Stage 3 disease are suitable for SBRT. Suitability is confirmed at consultation based on your MRI, PSA level, Gleason score, prostate size, and urinary symptoms.

SBRT for prostate cancer is covered under MediShield Life and Medisave, and most major Integrated Shield Plans. Cashless billing is available with 13 panel insurers. Our finance team will provide a written estimate of out-of-pocket costs before you commit to treatment.

Beyond SBRT, AARO offers conventional IMRT (35–40 sessions over 7–8 weeks) and brachytherapy (low-dose-rate seed implant or high-dose-rate temporary implant). Each has specific indications — your specialist will recommend the best fit for your case.

Modern linear accelerators (such as the TrueBeam at AARO's CSR facility) and CyberKnife are both capable of delivering SBRT. With current image-guidance and motion-management technology, outcomes are equivalent for prostate cancer. Treatment platform should not be your primary decision factor — specialist experience and personalised planning matter far more.

SBRT is generally well-tolerated. The most common short-term effects are urinary frequency and urgency, mild rectal irritation, and fatigue — all typically resolving within weeks. Long-term effects (rectal bleeding, urinary stricture, erectile dysfunction) are uncommon and lower than with surgery. SpaceOAR further reduces rectal-side risks.
