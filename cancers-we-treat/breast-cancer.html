Breast Cancer

Breast cancer is highly treatable when caught early.

It is the most common cancer in Singaporean women — and one with strong outcomes when diagnosed in time. AARO offers the full radiation toolkit: VMAT, SBRT for oligometastatic disease, brachytherapy boost, and accelerated partial-breast irradiation.

What is breast cancer

Understanding breast cancer

Breast cancer occurs when cells in the breast grow uncontrollably, forming a tumour. It can develop in different parts of the breast — most commonly the ducts (ductal carcinoma) or the lobules (lobular carcinoma). It is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in Singaporean women, and the leading cause of cancer death among women under 60.

Most breast cancers are detected through a combination of self-examination, routine mammography screening, and ultrasound. When detected at Stage 1 or 2, the prognosis is excellent — with five-year survival rates above 90%.

Symptoms

The most important breast cancer symptoms to watch for are:

  • A new lump or mass in the breast or underarm — usually painless, often hard, with irregular edges
  • Swelling or thickening of part of the breast
  • Skin dimpling, peeling, or redness — particularly if it resembles an orange peel
  • Nipple retraction, discharge (especially bloody), or scaling
  • Persistent breast or nipple pain that doesn't relate to your menstrual cycle
  • Change in size or shape of the breast

Many of these symptoms have benign causes — but any new or persistent change warrants a doctor's review and (usually) a mammogram or ultrasound.

What is a mammogram?

A mammogram is a low-dose X-ray of the breast — the most reliable screening tool for detecting breast cancer before it can be felt. Singapore's Health Promotion Board recommends:

  • Annual mammograms for women aged 40–49 (after counselling on the risks and benefits)
  • Mammograms every two years for women aged 50 and above
  • Earlier and more frequent screening for women with family history or known genetic risk (e.g., BRCA1/BRCA2)

If your mammogram identifies a suspicious area, the next step is typically a follow-up ultrasound and a needle biopsy to confirm whether the tissue is cancerous.

Who is at risk?

Risk factors for breast cancer include:

  • Being female (men can also develop breast cancer, but the risk is around 100× lower)
  • Increasing age — most cases occur in women over 50
  • Family history of breast or ovarian cancer, particularly in first-degree relatives
  • Inherited genetic mutations — BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, and others
  • Hormonal factors — early menstruation, late menopause, no children or first child after 30, hormone replacement therapy
  • Lifestyle — alcohol consumption, postmenopausal obesity, physical inactivity
  • Dense breast tissue and prior chest radiation (e.g., for childhood Hodgkin lymphoma)

Many women diagnosed with breast cancer have no identifiable risk factor — which is why regular screening matters even for those who feel low-risk.

How is breast cancer treated?

Breast cancer treatment is highly individual and typically combines multiple modalities. Your specific plan will depend on the stage, the tumour's hormone-receptor and HER2 status, and your overall health.

Surgery

Most breast cancers are treated with surgery — either lumpectomy (removing the tumour and a margin of healthy tissue) or mastectomy (removing the entire breast). Sentinel-node biopsy is usually performed at the same time to check whether cancer has spread.

Radiation therapy

Radiation typically follows lumpectomy and is sometimes used after mastectomy in higher-risk cases. AARO offers:

  • Whole-breast VMAT/IMRT — modern, precise external-beam radiation that treats the breast tissue while minimising dose to the heart and lungs
  • Accelerated Partial-Breast Irradiation (APBI) — a shorter course (1–3 weeks) targeting only the area around the original tumour, suitable for selected early-stage patients
  • Boost dose — additional radiation delivered to the tumour bed using IMRT or brachytherapy
  • Deep-Inspiration Breath-Hold (DIBH) — a technique that reduces heart-dose for left-sided breast cancer patients
  • SBRT — for oligometastatic disease (limited spread), where ablating each metastasis can improve outcomes

Systemic therapy

Depending on the cancer's biology, treatment may also include hormone (endocrine) therapy, HER2-targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or chemotherapy. Many AARO patients are managed jointly with a medical oncologist who leads the systemic component while our specialists lead the radiation component.
